![Volunteering](/public/assets/img/startup-weekend-volunteers.jpg)

Steve Blank is an entrusted figure within the Startup community. He tells four stories within his <a href="http://steveblank.com/2011/05/17/philadelphia-university-commencement-speech-%E2%80%93-may-15th-2011/" target="_blank">Philadelphia University commencement speech</a>. He focuses his second story around “Volunteering and Showing Up.”<br>

In that story, he relates about his time in the Air Force. He volunteered to go to Thailand during the Vietnam War, where he helped at the local on-base repair shop rather than going out drinking with his mates. He fixed broken electronics he knew little about and before the age of 20, was awarded responsibility over 15 electricians in the electronic warfare shop.

By showing up at the shop and volunteering his invaluable, though untrained time Steve Blank started his career trajectory into technology. A simple act which lead him to become the inspiration entrepreneurs look toward.

## Volunteering in San Diego

I didn’t learn about Steve Blank until I arrived in Melbourne. Before then, freshly out of University, I interned with the Tech Coast Angels in Orange County. There I was more of a hassle than a help to my supervisor. I learned a lot, had my first taste of entrepreneurship, and eventually won the opportunity to work for the San Diego network of Tech Coast Angels.

The real change in my entrepreneurial journey, came when the CTO of a San Diego startup suggested that I attend Hackathon Fridays at Ansir Innovation Centre. So I went. After attending a few times, I was regularly volunteering at the Ansir Innovation Centre.

That’s what I did for a year. I spoke to everyone I could, I helped out how I could. I found a group of passionate, innovative people while unwittingly stumbling upon my own passion.

Through showing up and volunteering, I realised how different volunteering was from an internship. It’s informal and for that reason, it’s amazing the diversity of opportunities you find.

## Showing up in Melbourne

Arriving in Melbourne, I didn’t know anyone, I needed to meet people. I started the process over.

I went to events I found interesting. Before the events, I contacted the organisers. When I arrived I introduced myself to _everyone_.

I asked people about the events they were attending, what they were reading, where they found out about events. For Melbourne, I found <a href="http://thefetch.com/" target="_blank">The Fetch</a>, <a href="https://www.startupdigest.com/" target="_blank">Startup Digest</a>, and <a href="http://StartupMelbourne.com" target="_blank"><span class="s3">StartupMelbourne.com</a>. Armed with this info, I had a full schedule of events.

I said in my head, “I want people to think, ‘Who the hell is that guy? Why do I see him everywhere?’” It was tiring, but that’s _Showing up_.

## Volunteering in Melbourne

_Asking to help_ is the difference.

Where I saw a volunteer button, I pressed it. When I emailed the organisers, I asked how I could help. When I showed up and met the organisers, I asked again.

That persistence is the competitive advantage in volunteering. Ask how you can help, then ask again, and again… you get the picture. Ask different people. Ask the organisers, ask the staff, ask the attendees, ask the person setting up, registering people, and cleaning up.

When you’re asking to help, you’re the person looking to get shit done. In return, you get an insider’s perspective of what’s happening at the event. Do it long enough and someone will think you’re in charge or they’ll put you in charge.

## Get results

Where did this methodology lead me? Everyone I worked with in the last 2 years, I met due to this habit.

It brought me to York Butter Factory and a fellowship with venture capital firm, Adventure Capital. I became the MC for Melbourne Silicon Beach Drinks and connected with a growing community of technologists and entrepreneurs. I participated in and organised numerous events for Startup Weekend Australia. Through volunteering, I began to work with award-winning startups, such as <a href="http://caremonkeyapp.com/" target="_blank">CareMonkey</a> and <a href="http://MMGN.com" target="_blank"><span class="s3">MMGN.com</a>.

Whatever I was doing, it was working.

## Lessons learned

Whatever your available time or preferred method, Professor Blank and I are both examples of why going out and trying your hand at volunteering, simply works.

To get started, here’s a simple primer of my tried and true game-plan.

### Find Events

Go to <a href="http://meetup.com/" target="_blank"><span class="s4">meetup.com</a>, <a href="http://eventbrite.com/" target="_blank"><span class="s4">eventbrite.com</a>, or a specific industry event website. Search for events related to your interest.

Choose 5 events in the next 60 days that you’re going to attend. Confirm your RSVP on the event page. Put it in your calendar.

“How can I help?”

Find the “Contact the Organiser” button. Email the organiser saying the following -

- That you’re excited to attend the event
- A little bit about you
- Why you’re interested in attending
- Then ask _how_ you can help.

If they say yes (woohoo!), you’ve accomplished your first step. Now _follow through_

If they say no or don’t respond, wait until the day before the event and ask again, letting them know you’re available 30 minutes before and after the event.

When you’re at the event, seek out the organisers. Thank them for organising the event and ask _how_ you can help. (If you think you’re getting a little annoying, that’s a good thing.)

### Follow up

After the event, contact them to thank them for organising. If you liked the event, ask _how_ you can help with the next one.

**Rinse and Repeat**

Simply put, it’s finding the events you like, then be annoying enough with how much you can help. At some point, they will give you something to do just to shut you up.

Best of luck.
