### _Learning to be a better programmer is never not hard._

There are lots of tips, tricks, tools, and tactics (woo alliteration!) to make the process of solving programming obstacles easier. I'm going to share one of these with you.

### ​*Timeboxing!*​

You can read a little bit more about it [here](http://guide.agilealliance.org/guide/timebox.html), but the TL;DR is:

Begin working on a problem, set a timer for X (15, 30, 45) minutes to which you will dedicate your time to solving this problem.

If after the timer finishes and you haven't solved the problem or overcome the obstacle, ask someone!`

### Oli's Hierarchy for Asking Programming Questions

Typically, my suggested hierarchy for asking a question regarding programming is the following:

1. ​**Ask the code** - Can you find an example of the problem you're trying to solve in other chunks of code that you have access to?

2. ​**Ask Stack Overflow**​ - If you're googling or stack overflowing and then trying things, congratulations you're a darn good programmer already. A lot of times answers will be online, either as part of a slack overflow answer (with caveats\*) or as part of a blog post.

3. ​**Ask your peer**​ - Ask your peer, either the person working through this issue next to you, or throw your question into slack where we will do our best to answer your questions

4. ​**Ask a pro**​ - Shoot a message to an expert, whether that's your co-worker, a professor, or even the person who wrote the codebase. You'd be surprised how often the person who wrote the original code will be nice enough to assist you or direct you to someone else who knows.

Hope that helps!
